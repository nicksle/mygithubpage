Background

Through the class IEOR 170: Industrial Desing and Human Factors, I was given the chance to work with a great group to tackle an issue that was dear to our hearts: food sustainability and food accessibility in the Bay Area.

The final deliverables that were required for the project were:

  • a functional prototype
  • a final presentation poster
  • a demonstration video

The Challenge

How might we create a product that is able to reduce food insecurity in East Bay communities, while also implementing a way to increase the sustainablilty of the produce.

We began our project wanting to address the problem of food insecurity and sustainability in the United States. The U.S. Department of Agriculture defines food security as "access by all people at all times to enough food for an active, healthy life.” Food insecurity is an issue faced daily by us, our peers, and our neighbors due to a lack of price inclusive, nutritious foods. This motivated us to understand the topic more and come up with creative solutions to help alleviate this pressing problem. Additionally, we wanted to be able to address food insecurity in a way that promoted food sustainability. When buying groceries, most shoppers don't consider the carbon footprint associated with the produce. Grocery store produce creates a large carbon footprint because of the amount of energy required to carry the produce from the farm to the store.

User Research and Data Analysis

For our user research we:

  • performed secondary research focusing specifically on
  • conducted interviews with residents of food insecure areas
  • created user personas and a user journey map based on the information gathered from user interviews

From the secondary research, we utalized online resources to discover that the most accessible ways to increase food sustainability would be: indoor hydroponics, farmers markets and community gardens. We created a pros and cons chat that highlighted the sustainability and accessibility aspects of each solution.

We set this data aside for the time being, then synthesized the results from our interviews. From our interviews we were able to deduce three main insights:

After taking the insights from our interviews in account, we used our pros and cons chart to deduce that community gardens were the solution for our users; primarily because:

  • they are available to most members of the community (but are not utilized due to lack of exposure)
  • have an extremely low carbon foot print
  • have a low actual monetary cost

With a new framework in mind, we conducted another set of interviews, this time specificially focusing on the users interactions with community gardens; we then broke down our users into three main groups (community garden manager, avid garden goers and average community members) and created user personas about them.


The personas allowed us to better empathize and understand who we are designing for. Some aspects of our users that stood out to us were:

  • Avid garden goers had a difficult time navigating the garden and felt that the lack of organization led to too much wasted time.
  • Garden managers became frustrated when overstocked produce was not harvested properly or not picked up, resulting in excessive wasted produce
  • Community members want to be able to utilize the community garden, but either don’t feel comfortable or are unaware of the logistics surrounding the garden

Following our user personas, we created an in-depth user journey that followed the steps that the user would typically take on a trip to their community garden. We hoped that the mapped out user journey would allow us to see exactly where users felt most stressed as well as what aspects of commynity gardens we could build up upon.

Our user journey map was extremely helpful in pinpointing exactly which experiences could be approved upon. We deduced that the primary sources of frustration faced by the community members were:

  • a difficult time enteringand engagingthe community garden because of unfamiliarity
  • a messy task board made finding and executing tasks excessively difficult
  • a cluttered event board resulted in community events being overlooked and difficult to attend

Taking all our data into account, our team deduced 4 main user needs that we could use as a basis for our design solution:

  1.   Increased community engagement and outreach
  2.   A more connected information regarding produce stock
  3.   A more clarified task and event board
  4.   A learning center for gardening skills

Ideation

For our ideation phase, we used the user needs as design opportunities to discovered from our research to address pain points or design opportunities from our user journey, or reach the goals and alleviate the frustrations that we were stated in our user personas.

At the conclusion of our ideation phase, we ended up with many separate solutions that would allow our users to better utalize their community, and in turn increase their food sustainability. Our team decided that the next step would to be to create one solution that included all these separated ideas into a cohesive and easy to use design. We tinkered around with different ideas of physical and digital products and decided that the most feasible designs would be:

  • systems redesign
  • concept app design

Systems Redesign

The systems redesign of the community garden would put a larger focus on the community aspect of the garden. It would be more than just members picking up produce; the garden would be a hub of activity that excites the community to partake in the gardening. To execute this, we suggested that:

  • the community managers plan more community events
  • managers spend time highlighting certain community members every week
  • the garden be restructured into a grid based system that streamlines the process of finding produce.

Concept App Design

A mobile app was the easiest way to implement core features that attended to user needs. A mobile app provides:

  • an accessible medium for users to see what produce are available
  • the ability to see what task need to be completed in the garden
  • the capability to provide live time updates on vital information such as
    • change in produce stocks,
    • completion of tasks
    • upcoming events

The Solution: Introducing WeGrow

WeGrow is a concept app that helps community members familiarize themselves with their local community garden. The app's core functionality is catered towards assiting garden goers with locating their desired produce in a timely manner. Additionally, WeGrow acts to keep the community up to date with the community happenings, through a digital task / event board. Ultimately, WeGrow aims to increase participation and access to these local gardens, and promote a sustainable and accessible way of consuming.

Personalized Dashboard & Featured Content

A centralized dashboard allows community members to see personalized information such as when their favorite produce is available, upcoming events and recommended tasks. Additionally, the dashboard promotes community engagement with recommended and favorited content, allowing users to see their current interactions with the garden. Another large component of the dashboard is the promotion of featured content including the featured produce and featured community member. The inclusion of these featured content is a form of community outreach and acts to instill excitement towards the community garden.

Intuitive Color Scheme

A key design choice for the app was to use an intuitive color scheme to help guide users through the app. The implementation of a familiar “stop-light” color scheme allows users to easily understand the status of things without the need to see further text. Within the task board feature, WeGrow uses the color scheme to convey the urgency of a certain task and to highlight the status of an event. Within the picking produce feature, the color indicates the status and stock of a certain produce.

Picking Produce

With the picking produce feature, users can easily see which produce are currently available at the garden and can identify their stock and condition using the color indicators. If a produce is high in stock it will indicated with green, moderate stock is yellow, and low stock is red. WeGrow also introduces a low burden method for users to updates on stock and condition though the use of colored sliders. The sliders have a similar color scheme as in the main produce page, allowing users to understand that green means good, yellow moderate, and red low/bad.

Streamlined Task Assignments & Event Board

The Task Board/Event Board tab consolidates all the tasks and events that are ongoing at the garden. This centralized view of the garden tasks easily allows users to see what needs to be done at the garden and provides the ability to update what work has been done in the real time. By clicking on a tasks, users can see more information about the task such as where it takes place, what skills and items are needed and when it needs to be completed. Tasks are color coded to help users understand the level of urgency of the tasks: green means low-urgency, yellow moderate, and red urgent. Additionally, users can view upcoming community events. Events that have not been RSVP’d to are displayed as yellow; events that are marked as “going” are green, and red is used to indicate “not going”.

Gaining Gardening Skills

The “Learning” feature of the app consists of modules that allows users to learn the basics of gardening. After choosing a module, users can click on a skill and watch a video on how to perform a specific action. After the skill has been “learned” and marked as done, garden managers will be informed and users will being to be recommended tasks that require that skill. “Learned” skills are be reviewed and will be marked as completed to help users keep track of what they have learned.
